Emotional Intelligence

EQ vs IQ

We all know about IQ. It's based on logic and how good you are at solving abstract problems. IQ is static - your level of intelligence is determined at birth. IQ is an old concept of what intelligence is. Researchers interested in how our minds work felt that IQ wasn't sufficient by itself to analyse how intelligent a person is and they started to look at other types of intelligence, one of these being emotional intelligence (EQ).

Emotional intelligence looks at how well you understand yourself (your strengths and weaknesses, how you react emotionally in difficult situations) and how well you understand other people. EQ is seen as neuroplastic - we can improve it with practice, it's not set at birth.

Why is EQ important?

EQ is important because it is interpersonal. We don't work in a bubble. We have to be aware of how other people are feeling and how we're feeling, so that we can change our behaviour when it's negative or unproductive and so that we can relate to the people we work with. If we want to take on a leadership role, we will need to understand and be able to relate well to the people that we are leading. EQ also helps us deal with challenges in life. Life is not predictable or easy.

If EQ is changable, how can we improve it?

  1. Changing our brain patterns with meditation. The point of meditation is to be in the present. If you train your brain to be in the present, you'll be able to recognise your emotions as they happen and be able to practice active listening to others (not thinking about other things while someone else is talking).
  2. Recognising our emotions by paying attention to how our bodies feel (our physiological reactions) and by stopping for a moment before reacting. When I get anxious, my hands get sweaty and my shoulders stiffen. It can be easier to notice these traits than the emotion itself. Once I've recognised the emotion, once I know what's going on, I can take a moment to breathe and try to understand why it's happening.
  3. Understanding ourselves and our emotions. If I have the same kind of emotional response to a particular trigger, and I can recognise that trigger before reacting, I can try to change my behaviour. For example, if I know that if I get overstimulated because I'm trying to concentrate on a task and someone is talking to me at the same time, I can get irrationally irritated. When I feel myself getting frustrated, I can take a step back before I react out of anger and snap at someone. I can then narrow down my focus and ask that person to wait until I finish my task or thought process, or stop what I'm doing to listen.
  4. Understanding other people. Actively listening to other people can help us understand where someone else is coming from. If we are quiet when someone else is talking and don't interject, if we concentrate on being in the moment and listening rather than thinking about what we want for lunch, we don't direct the conversation but instead let the person explain themselves completely. When we repeat back what we think we heard, they can either feel understood or correct us if we've missed something.
